Pathogen & Disease

Pathogen: Mycobacterium tuberculosis
Disease: Tuberculosis (TB)

How It Causes Disease

Tuberculosis (TB) spreads when someone breathes in tiny droplets from an infected person’s cough or sneeze. Once the bacteria reach the lungs, M. tuberculosis can start multiplying and hide from the immune system by taking shelter inside immune cells called macrophages. (CDC, 2023)

Symptoms

People with TB often experience a lingering cough, fever, night sweats, weight loss, and constant tiredness. In more serious cases, they might even cough up blood. (WHO, 2022)

Immune Response

When M. tuberculosis enters the body, the immune system fights back by sending in macrophages and T-cells. These cells form clumps called granulomas to trap the bacteria. Even then, the bacteria can sometimes stay hidden in the body without causing symptoms—this is called latent TB. (Mayo Clinic, 2023)

Treatment

Treating TB takes time and consistency. Doctors usually prescribe a mix of antibiotics like isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol that need to be taken for several months to fully clear the infection. (CDC, 2023)

Prevention

To help prevent TB, people can get the BCG vaccine, wear masks in high-risk areas, and make sure indoor spaces have good airflow. Catching and treating latent TB early is also a key part of stopping its spread. (WHO, 2022)
